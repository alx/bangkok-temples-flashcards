### 1. Wat Phra Kaew (Emerald Buddha Temple)
- **Thai Name**: วัดพระแก้ว
- **Coordinates**: 13.6533°N, 100.4955°E
- **Founded**: 1782 (during Chakri Dynasty establishment)
- **Architectural Style**: Grand Palace Complex (Rattanakosin Thai)
- **Key Features**: 
  - Houses the Emerald Buddha (Phra Kaew Morakot) - most sacred object in Thailand
  - Ornate chedis (stupas), golden temple buildings
  - Royal temple of Thai kings
- **Historical Significance**: 
  - Constructed immediately after Bangkok was established as capital
  - Houses Thailand's most revered religious object
  - Represents the unity of Thai Buddhism and monarchy
  - The only Buddhist temple in Thailand where women cannot ordain as monks
- **Description**: 
Wat Phra Kaew, the Temple of the Emerald Buddha, stands as Thailand's most sacred Buddhist site within the Grand Palace complex. Founded in 1782 when Bangkok became the capital, this stunning Rattanakosin-style temple houses the Phra Kaew Morakot (Emerald Buddha), the most venerated religious object in Thailand despite measuring only 66 centimeters tall. The temple showcases extraordinary Thai craftsmanship with elaborate golden decorations, intricate mosaics, and ornate chedis. The Emerald Buddha is actually carved from jade and depicts the Buddha in the "Touching the Earth" pose. As a royal temple, it remains the most important religious site in the Thai kingdom, serving as both a spiritual center and symbol of Thai national identity and Buddhist faith.

### 2. Wat Pho (Temple of the Reclining Buddha)
- **Thai Name**: วัดโพธิ์
- **Coordinates**: 13.6462°N, 100.4912°E
- **Founded**: 16th century (originally), significantly renovated 1787-1832
- **Architectural Style**: Ayutthaya (Sukhothai influence, renovated Rattanakosin)
- **Key Features**: 
  - Giant Reclining Buddha statue (46 meters long, 15 meters high)
  - 8 chedis, one enclosing ashes of former Thai kings
  - Four viharas (assembly halls) with Buddha images
  - Famous for traditional Thai massage school
- **Historical Significance**: 
  - One of Bangkok's oldest temples, founded before Ayutthaya's fall
  - Major pilgrimage site throughout Thai history
  - Reclining Buddha represents Buddha entering Nirvana
  - Center of Thai traditional medicine knowledge preservation
- **Description**:
Wat Pho, the Temple of the Reclining Buddha, stands as one of Bangkok's most iconic Buddhist temples and a world-renowned pilgrimage destination. The temple's most famous feature is its colossal Reclining Buddha statue, measuring 46 meters long and standing 15 meters high. Cast in stucco and gilded in gold leaf, this Buddha image represents Siddhartha Gautama at the moment of entering Nirvana. Originally founded in the 16th century and significantly renovated during the early Chakri Dynasty (1787-1832), Wat Pho showcases Ayutthaya architectural traditions alongside later Thai additions. The temple complex spans 8 hectares and contains four main viharas (assembly halls), multiple chedis, and serves as an important center for Thai traditional medicine. Inside the Reclining Buddha's feet are 108 mother-of-pearl inlaid panels depicting scenes from Buddhist teachings.

### 3. Wat Saket (Temple of the Golden Mount)
- **Thai Name**: วัดสะเก็ด
- **Coordinates**: 13.6612°N, 100.5145°E
- **Founded**: 17th century Ayutthaya period, rebuilt 1807
- **Architectural Style**: Ayutthaya Thai with later additions
- **Key Features**: 
  - Golden Mount (Saket) - artificial hill 60+ meters high
  - Mondop (reliquary) at summit containing Gautama Buddha's relics
  - Views of entire Bangkok from summit
  - Annual festival (Visakha Bucha) lantern celebrations
- **Historical Significance**: 
  - Historic site dating to Ayutthaya kingdom
  - Landmark visible across Bangkok for centuries
  - Important pilgrimage destination (visitation by Thai kings common)
  - Site of annual Loy Kratip (Worshipping the Relics) festival
- **Description**:
Wat Saket, known as the Temple of the Golden Mount, features one of Bangkok's most distinctive landmarks: an artificial golden-topped hill rising over 60 meters above the surrounding city. Originally constructed during the Ayutthaya period and rebuilt in 1807, this historic temple complex includes a mondop (reliquary) at the summit containing relics believed to be from Gautama Buddha himself. The winding staircase ascending the mount offers panoramic views across Bangkok and the Chao Phraya River. Devotees climb this sacred hill as an act of Buddhist merit-making, with the journey itself considered spiritually significant. Annually, Wat Saket hosts the spectacular Loy Kratip Festival, where thousands of Bangkok residents gather to make merit, light candles, and release illuminated paper lanterns into the night sky—a breathtaking display of faith visible across the city.

### 5. Wat Arun (Temple of the Dawn)
- **Thai Name**: วัดอรุณ
- **Coordinates**: 13.6434°N, 100.4877°E
- **Founded**: 17th century Ayutthaya period, rebuilt 1809 (Rattanakosin)
- **Architectural Style**: Khmer-influenced (prang design), Thai adaptation
- **Key Features**: 
  - Central prang (tower) rising 86.8 meters
  - Four smaller corner prangs decorated with porcelain
  - Multi-tiered base with intricate decorative elements
  - Riverside location on Thonburi side of Chao Phraya
- **Historical Significance**: 
  - Major temple since Ayutthaya period
  - Associated with dawn worship (hence "Temple of the Dawn")
  - Rebuilt during reign of King Rama II
  - Iconic Bangkok landmark visible from both riverbanks
- **Description**:
Wat Arun, the Temple of the Dawn, stands as one of Southeast Asia's most visually distinctive Buddhist temples. Its central prang (Khmer-style tower) rises majestically to 86.8 meters, adorned with colorful porcelain pieces—a technique called "kranok" decoration that shimmers in changing light. Originally built during the Ayutthaya period and significantly rebuilt in 1809 during King Rama II's reign, Wat Arun embodies over three centuries of Buddhist architectural tradition. The name derives from the Hindu god Aruna, associated with dawn, as the temple's eastern orientation captures the morning sun spectacularly. The riverside location on Thonburi's bank of the Chao Phraya River makes it an iconic Bangkok landmark—dramatically lit at night and reflected in the river waters. Visitors ascending the steep steps of the central prang are rewarded with panoramic views of Bangkok and the Chao Phraya River.

### 6. Wat Traimit (Temple of the Golden Buddha)
- **Thai Name**: วัดไตรมิตร
- **Coordinates**: 13.6270°N, 100.5120°E
- **Founded**: 13th century (Sukhothai period), relocated to Yaowarat 1911
- **Architectural Style**: Chinese shophouse adapted as temple (Yaowarat area)
- **Key Features**: 
  - Golden Buddha statue (Phra Buddha Maha Suwanna) - 5.5 meters tall, 5.5 tons pure gold
  - Remarkable historical rediscovery (hidden under plaster for 200 years)
  - Located within Chinese shrine/temple complex
  - Modern museum documenting Buddha statue's history
- **Historical Significance**: 
  - Golden Buddha discovered intact in 1954 after centuries hidden
  - Represents intersection of Chinese and Thai Buddhist traditions
  - Located in historic Yaowarat (Chinese quarter) of Bangkok
  - Statue is world's largest golden Buddha from Sukhothai period
- **Description**:
Wat Traimit, the Temple of the Golden Buddha, houses one of Buddhism's most remarkable treasures—a massive Sukhothai-period Buddha statue cast from 5.5 tons of solid gold. Standing 5.5 meters high, this serene golden Buddha was cast during Thailand's 13th-century Sukhothai kingdom and remained hidden beneath a stucco covering for approximately 200 years, surviving wars and temple destructions. In 1954, during relocation to a new building in Bangkok's Yaowarat district, the stucco accidentally cracked, revealing the gleaming gold beneath. This extraordinary discovery captivated global attention and restored the statue to prominence. Today, Wat Traimit sits within a Chinese-style shophouse complex in Bangkok's historic Chinese quarter, reflecting the unique blend of Chinese and Thai Buddhist traditions in urban Bangkok. A modern museum chronicles the statue's fascinating history, from its Sukhothai creation through its hidden centuries to its dramatic rediscovery.

The bronze Buddha statue, cast using traditional methods, stands in serene meditation pose, gazing toward the river. Wat Suthat remains an important pilgrimage site and symbol of Buddhist devotion in Bangkok.
